\section{Introduction} \label{sc:intro}
% =============================================================================


Accurate mortality risk profiling in the Intensive Care Unit (ICU) is essential for clinical decision-making, resource allocation. Essentially all survival models rely on the independence censoring assumption, that is, the censoring mechanism (e.g., hospital discharge) carries no information about the underlying survival process beyond observed covariates \citep{kalbfleisch2003statistical}. In the ICU, this assumption is immediately suspect. Patient discharge is a purposeful clinical decision, exactly dependent on patient health status, creating a dependence structure (of censoring \(C_i\) and failure time \(T_i\)), violating the standard assumption, and biases hazard estimates and covariate effects.

\citet{tsiatis1975} Establishes a fundamental identifiability issue: from the observed data \((y_i, \delta_i)\) alone, marginals of \(T_i\) and \(C_i\) cannot be separately identified without structural assumptions on their joint distribution. Diagnostics too, cannot distinguish informative from non-informative censoring, motivating the need for sensitivity analyses.

This paper addresses the central question: \emph{how robust are Bayesian posterior inferences about ICU mortaliy risk fators to departures from the independent censoring assumption?} We make three contributions within the discrete-time framework. First, we formalize the independent censoring assumption in the missing data taxonomy, deriving explicit bias formulas for hazard estimation and covariate effects (Section~\ref{sc:naive-likelihood}, Appendix~\ref{app:biased-hazard}). Second, we develop a sensivity analysis following \citep{scharfstein1999} by introducing \(\gamma\) that indexes the departure from MAR, identifying \emph{tipping points} where clinical conclusions are changed (Section~\ref{sc:sensitivity}). Third, we compare this with a shared frailty model (Section~\ref{sc:frailty}) and demonstrate through both simulation (Appendix~\ref{app:simulation}) and application to 57,694 MIMIC-III patients (Section~\ref{sc:application}) that frailty is weakly identified and that sensitivity analysis is the more principled approach to account for informative censoring in this context.

\section{Data Structure \& Structure}\label{sc:notation}
% =============================================================================

% expand upon this for write-up
Let $i = 1, \dots, N$ index unique ICU admissions from MIMIC-III. We define \(T_i > 0\) as latent time to death, \(C_i > 0 \) as thhe censoring time (i.e., time of hospital discharge), and \(y_i = \min(T_i, C_i)\) as the observed follow-up time. The failure indicator is \(\delta_i = \ind(T_i \le C_i)\), indicates whether the patient died in the hospital (\(\delta_i = 1\)) or was discharged alive (\(\delta_i = 0\)), in days. And \(\bx_i \in \mathbb{R}^P\) is a vector of static baseline covariates measured at ICU admission. The observed data for patient \(i\) is thus \((y_i, \delta_i, \bx_i)\). The data covariate characteristics are described in more detail in Section~\ref{sc:application}, and Table~\ref{tab:covariates}.

\subsubsection{Discretization.}

We partition continuous time into discrete intervals of width \(\Delta\): The intervals are \([0,\Delta), [\Delta, 2\Delta), \dots, [(K-1)\Delta, K\Delta)\) with \(t = 1, \dots, K\). The discrete failure time is \(\tilde{T}_i = \lceil T_i / \Delta \rceil\) and similarly, \(\tilde{C}_i = \lceil C_i / \Delta \rceil\). Our primary analysis uses daily discretization (\(\Delta = 1\) day), over 30-day window (\(K = 30\)), motivated by the standard use of 30-day ICU outcome metric, the median follow-up of \(\approx 6\) days in MIMIC-III, so the window captures the clinically relevant risk period. We will proceed by dropping the tildes for notational simplicity, so \(T_i\) and \(C_i\) refer to the discretized times. 

Our simulation study shows that covariate effect estimates are stable across \(\Delta = \{1, 3, 7, 14\}\) (Appendix~\ref{app:simulation}). The choice of \(\Delta\) involves a bias--variance trade-off: finer discretization provides a closer approximation to the underlying continuous-time process, but producing a larger dataset and more interval-specific parameters to estimate, which can lead to finite-sample instability. 

\subsubsection{Person-Period expansion.}
Following \citep{allison-discrete}, the data is reconstructed into a ``person-period'' format, so each patient \(i\) contributes one row per interval \(t = 1, \dots, K_i\) where \(K_i = \min(T_i, C_i)\) is the last interval for which patient \(i\) is at risk. At time \(t = K_i\), the binary outcome is \(y_{i,K_i} = \ind \left( \delta_i = 1 \right)\), is either 1 if patient \(i\) dies, or 0 if censored at time \(t = K_i\). These are absorbing states, so patients exit the risk set thereafter. Considering who is still under observation at each time \(t\) is critical to further analysis, as the risk set at time \(t\) is \(\mathcal{R}_t = \{i: y_i \ge t\} = \{i: T_i \ge t, C_i \ge t\}\) and the censored set is \(\mathcal{C}_t = \{i: C_i < t\}\).

\subsection{Standard Likelihood \& Censoring Assumption} \label{sc:naive-likelihood}

The discrete-time hazard for patient \(i\) at time \(t\) is \(h_{i,t} = P(T_i = t \mid T_i \geq t, \mathbf{x}_i)\), with the corresponding survival function \(S_i(t) = P(T_i > t \mid \mathbf{x}_i) = \prod_{s=1}^t (1 - h_{i,s})\). The full data likelihood involves the joint density of \((y_i, \delta_i)\) depending both on failure and censoring parameters \(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \boldsymbol{\phi}\) respectively. \citep[Section 3.2]{kalbfleisch2003statistical}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:full-lik}
    \mathcal{L}_i^{\text{full}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \boldsymbol{\phi})
    = \bigl[f_T(y_i \mid \bx_i)\, S_C(y_i \mid \bx_i)\bigr]^{\delta_i}
      \bigl[S_T(y_i \mid \bx_i)\, f_C(y_i \mid \bx_i)\bigr]^{1 - \delta_i}
\end{equation}

\begin{assumption}[][Independent Right-Censoring; K\&P Section~2.4]
  \label{assump:indep-cens}
  $T_i \perp C_i \mid \bx_i$: knowing the censoring time provides no additional information about survival beyond $\bx_i$.
\end{assumption}

\noindent
Under Assumption~\ref{assump:indep-cens}, \eqref{eq:full-lik} factors so that inference about $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ proceeds without modeling censoring. In person-period form: \(\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \prod_{i=1}^N \prod_{t=1}^{K_i} h_{i,t}^{y_{i,t}} (1 - h_{i,t})^{1 - y_{i,t}}\),
where each $(i,t)$ term is an independent Bernoulli trial. This corresponds to Missing at Random (MAR) in \citet{rubin1976}'s taxonomy. Since time-varying information is likely critical to drive discharge decisions, and thus the censoring mechanism is likely informative (i.e., \(T_i \not\perp C_i \mid \bx_i\)), the MAR assumption is likely violated in this context, motivating the methods below.

\subsection{Consequences of Informative Censoring}
When the independent censoring assumption fails, two consequences follow. First, systematic discharge of healthier patients inflates the observed hazard. Where \(\lambda_t = |\mathcal{C}_t|/|\mathcal{R}_t|\) is the proportion of patients censored at \(t\), and \(\Delta_t = h_t^\ast - \hat{h}_t\) is the difference between the true marginal hazard \(h_t^\ast\) and the observed hazard \(\hat{h}_t\). Then Appendix~\ref{app:biased-hazard}:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:bias-approx}
    \hat{h}_t \approx h_t^\ast + \frac{\lambda_t \Delta_t }{1-\lambda_t} \quad \text{where} \quad
    \hat{h}_t = \frac{ \sum_{i \in \mathcal{R}_t} \ind(T_i = t) }{ |\mathcal{R}_t| }
\end{equation}
This bias is positive whenever, \(\lambda_t\) and \(\Delta_t > 0\) (i.e., censored patients are healthier). But \(\Delta_t\) could be negative indicating censored patients are sicker, which would yield a negative bias term. This is revisited in Subsection~\ref{subsec:justification}. 

Second, covariate information that inform early discharge and lower mortality risk (e.g., elective admission, lower comorbidity scores) will have their effects \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) confounded by the  censoring process. \citep{scharfstein1999} formalizes this as a failure of the semiparametric estimation equations when MAR is violated, where the score function for \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) is biased proportional to the dependence between \(T_i\) and \(C_i\) given \(\bx_i\) (the selection bias). This is formalized in Appendix~\ref{app:scharfstein1999}, where the bias in the score function for \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) under informative censoring can be expressed as: 
\begin{equation}  \label{eq:confounding-bias}
    \text{Bias}(\alpha) \approx -\alpha \, \mathbb{E} \Big[ \Lambda_0(C_i \mid \bx_i) \, T_i \, S_\text{full}(\boldsymbol{\beta}) \Big]
\end{equation}
In the context of this project, the full-data score (\(S_\text{full}(\boldsymbol{\beta})\)) is Equation~\ref{eq:full-lik}. This bias is driven by \(\alpha\) (the severity of the MAR violation/selection) and the covariance (cumulative hazard) between the failure time \(T_i\) and the censoring \(\Lambda_0(C_i \mid \bx_i)\). Because \(\alpha\) cannot be identified from the observed data alone, standard estimation fails motivating the sensitivity analysis and frailty model. 

Though the frailty model sought to absorb the heterogeneity the drive censoring and failure, our demo simulation demonstrates this approach faces identifiability challenges. The primary model, Model A, is given below and compared against the frailty extension, Model B.

\subsection{Logistic Hazard Model} \label{sc:model}
% =============================================================================

The persion-period formulation recasts the discrete-time survival hazard as a first-order Markov chain on the state space \(\{1,0\}\) where \(0\) denotes ``alive'' and \(1\) denotes ``dead'' (an absorbing state). At each interval \(t\), a patient who survives to that point (\(y_{i,t-1} = 0\)) either transitons to death (\(y_{i,t} = 1\)) or remains alive (\(y_{i,t} = 0\)). Conditioning on previous interval \(y_{i,t-1} = 0\), requires that all preceding intervals are also zero. The discrete-time hazard precisely the one-step transition probability, which can be modeled as a Bernoulli GLMM with a logistic link. 

\subsection{Standard Model (Model A)}  \label{sc:standard-model} 
The primary analysis model specifies the hazard via a logistic link within interval-specific intercepts and shared covariate effects:
\begin{align}
    y_{i,t} \mid h_{i,t} &\sim \text{Bernoulli}(h_{i,t}), \quad t = 1, \dots, K_i,\; i = 1, \dots, N, \label{eq:obs-model} \\
    \text{logit}(h_{i,t}) &= \alpha_t + \bx_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta} 
    \label{eq:link-standard}
\end{align}
with priors:
\begin{alignat}{2}
    \alpha_1 &\sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_0^2), &\qquad&\text{(anchor)} \label{eq:alpha1-prior} \\
    \alpha_t \mid \alpha_{t-1} &\sim \mathcal{N}(\alpha_{t-1}, \sigma_\alpha^2), &\qquad& t = 2, \dots, K, \label{eq:rw-prior} \\
    \sigma_\alpha &\sim \text{Student-}t^+(\text{df=}3, 0, 1), \label{eq:sigma-alpha-prior} \\
    \boldsymbol{\beta} &\sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \tau^2 \mathbf{I}_P). \label{eq:beta-prior}
\end{alignat}

The first-order random walk prior on \(\boldsymbol{\alpha} = (\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_K)^\top\) encodes the temporal correlaition and the belief that neighboring intervals have similar baseline log-odds of death. As the risk set shrinks at later intervals, this prior borrows strength from adjacent time points, regularizing \(\alpha_t\) where data is sparse. The half Student-\(t\) prior on \(\sigma_\alpha\) is weakly informative and avoids the boundary issues of conjugate Inverse-Gamma priors with small shape parameters \citep{gelman-prior-2006}. We set \(\sigma_0^2 = \tau^2 = 10\) as weakly informative defaults, allowing shrinkage of both \(\alpha_t)\) and \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) towards zero when data is sparse, while still allowing for large effects when supported by the data. Let \(\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\boldsymbol{\alpha}, \boldsymbol{\beta}) \in \mathbb{R}^{K + P}\). Appendix~\ref{app:simulation} guides justification here, showing well-identified and convergent under under HMC, with ESS in the thousands for all \(\beta_j\) and no divergences.

\subsection{Frailty Extension (Model B)} \label{sc:frailty}
To capture unobserved patient-level heterogeneity, we extend Model A with a Gaussain random effect \(b_i\) (frailty) for each patient \(i\) to account for the \emph{within} patient correlation across intervals:
\begin{align}
    \text{logit}(h_{i,t}) &= \alpha_t + \bx_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta} + b_i 
    \label{eq:link-frailty}
\end{align}
with additional priors:
\begin{alignat}{2}
    b_i &\sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_b^2), &\qquad& i = 1, \dots, N,\label{eq:frailty-prior} \\
    \sigma_b &\sim \text{Exp}(1). \label{eq:sigma-b-prior}
\end{alignat}
the penalized complexity (PC) prior as a standard exponential, favoring smaller \(\sigma_b^2\) \citep{simpson-pc-priors} which shrinks towards no heterogeneity \(b_i = 0\) which is simply Model A. This is more principled than the Inverse-Gamma hyperprior for this setting, as it avoids both the small shape parameter placing excessive mass near zero and having heavy right tails. 


\subsubsection{Justification \& Limitations.} \label{subsec:justification}
To account for unobserved heterogeneity that may drive both censoring and failure, we introduce a patient-specific frailty term \(b_i\) in the linear predictor. By conditioning on \(b_i\), the model relaxes the strict independence assumption \eqref{assump:indep-cens} to a strictly weaker condition \eqref{eq:frailty-indep-cens}, requiring only the augmented covariate set \((\bx_i, b_i)\):

\begin{equation} \label{eq:frailty-indep-cens}
    T_i \perp C_i \;\mid\; \bx_i, b_i
\end{equation}
An important caveat is that addition of \(b_i\) does not guarentee \eqref{eq:frailty-indep-cens} holds, as the frailty is a misspecified proxy for the true unobserved confounder(s) that drive both censoring and failure. If \(b_i\) captures some (not all) of the relevant heterogeneity, then there will still be residual informative censoring bias. This can be though of as a partial correction to \eqref{eq:bias-approx}, where the bias is attenuated by the degree to which \(b_i\) captures heterogeneity. By individually estimating these extra \(N\) random effects, it allows for censoring of either improving (\(b_i < 0\)) or deteriorating (\(b_i > 0\)) patients, which would yield a positive/negative bias term in \eqref{eq:bias-approx} respectively. However, the downside is that it significantly increases the dimensionality of the estimation problem, and thus identifiability becomes a major concern, plummeting ESS for \(\sigma_b^2\) and \(b_i\) in practice.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis via Exponential Tilting} \label{sc:sensitivity}
%==============================================================================

The sensitivity analysis on Model A (only) and frailty model address complementatry aspects of informative censoring. The random effect \(b_i\) captures some heterogenity \emph{parametrically}, assuming the Gaussian prior \eqref{eq:frailty-prior} and condition \eqref{eq:frailty-indep-cens}. The sensitivity analysis, by contrast, \emph{nonparametrically} aims to index the departures from independent censoring without specification of a particular dependency structure.

Following \citet{scharfstein1999} and the Bayesian treatment of \citet{daniels2008}, define \(\gamma\) as the log-odds ratio of death at time \(T_i = t\) between patients censored at \(t\) and those remaining under observation at \(t\):
\begin{equation}
    \gamma = \log\left( \frac{\Pr( T_i = t \mid T_i \geq t, C_i = t, \bx_i)}{\Pr( T_i = t \mid T_i \geq t, C_i > t, \bx_i)} \right)
\end{equation}
Here, \(\gamma = 0\) corresponds to MAR, \(\gamma < 0\) means censored patients have lower mortality risk (discharge reflects improvement), and \(\gamma > 0\) means censored patients have higher mortality risk (discharge reflects transfer to hospice). \(\gamma\) is equivalent of the selection bias parameter ``\(\alpha\)'' in \eqref{eq:confounding-bias}, indexing the residual selection bias that cannot be captured by \(\bx_i\) alone. By sweeping \(\gamma\) over a grid, we can assess how sensitive our inferences are to departures from the MAR assumption, without parametric assumptions or identifiability issues. Note that we conduct the sensitivity analysis on Model A and do not condition on \(b_i\), so \(\gamma\) indexes the \emph{total} departure from MAR, rather than the \emph{residual} departure beyond what the frailty captures.


\subsubsection{Tilted Hazard \& Likelihood.}
The tilted hazard \(\tilde{h}_{i,t} = P(T_i = t \mid T_i \geq t, \bx_i, \gamma)\) adjusts the censored observations at the terminal interval:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tilted-hazard}
    \mathrm{logit}(\tilde{h}_{i,t}) = \mathrm{logit}(h_{i,t}) + \gamma \ind(C_i = t, \delta_i = 0), \qquad \text{ where } \delta_i = \ind(T_i \le C_i)
\end{equation}

Equivalently, the mortality odds are multiplied by \(\exp(\gamma)\) at the censoring interval. For uncensored observations and non-terminal intervals, \(\tilde{h}_{i,t} = h_{i,t}\). The tilted likelihood is:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:tilted-lik}
    \tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}; \gamma) = \prod_{i=1}^N \prod_{t=1}^{K_i} \tilde{h}_{i,t}^{y_{i,t}} (1 - \tilde{h}_{i,t})^{1 - y_{i,t}}
\end{equation}

This reweights the original likelihood, where their contribution of censored observations at their terminal interval is adjusted by \(\gamma\). Since \(\gamma\) is fixed and the censoring indicator is observed, it is simply Model A with an offet at censoring time: 
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{logit}(\tilde{h}_{i,t}) = \alpha_t + \bx_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta} + o_{i,t}
\end{equation}
with tilt term \(o_{i,t} = \gamma \ind(C_i = t, \delta_i = 0)\). Unlike \(\sigma_b^2\), \(\gamma\) is swept over a grid rather than being estimated, making the analysis immune to identifiability issues. The \emph{tipping point} \(\gamma^\ast_j\) for covariate \(j\) is the value within the 95\% CrI for \(\beta_j\) first includes zero, quantifying the degree of MAR violation required to change clinical conclusions about covariate \(j\).

\subsection{Protocol.}

Following \citet{daniels2008}:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep, topsep=0pt]
    \item Fit the standard model (Model~A) with $\gamma = 0$ and record posterior
      summaries of $\boldsymbol{\beta}$, $\sigma_\alpha$, and $\hat{S}(t \mid \bx)$.
    \item Select a grid
      $\gamma \in \{-2.0, -1.5, -1.0, -0.5, -0.25, 0, 0.25, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$, where
      negative values correspond to the clinically plausible
      direction (healthier patients discharged earlier).
    \item For each $\gamma$, refit Model~A with the tilted likelihood~\eqref{eq:tilted-lik}.
    \item Report posterior means and 95\% credible intervals for each $\beta_j$
      as a function of $\gamma$ (sensitivity plot).
    \item Identify the tipping point $\gamma^\ast_j$ for each covariate: the value at which the
      95\% credible interval for $\beta_j$ first includes zero.
\end{enumerate}

If conclusions are robust across the grid of \(\gamma\), the standard analysis assuming MAR is sufficient, despite the potential for informative censoring. If conclusions change at modest values of \(|\gamma|\), this quantifies the fragility of the findings.

\section{Computation} \label{sc:computation}
% =============================================================================
We initially developed a P\'olya-Gamma (PG) Gibbs sampler \citep{polya-gamma} for the logistic hazard model to leverage conjugacy for efficient posterior computation. However, the centered parameterization of the frailty model exhibited Neal's funnel geometry \citep{neal-funnel}, for small $\sigma_b^2$ the frailties $b_i$ mixed well, but for larger values the sampler failed to explore the posterior due to the strong posterior correlation, yielding very low ESS. This motivated the switch to Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) via Stan \citep{stan-software}.

\subsection{Hamiltonian Monte Carlo via Stan}\label{sc:stan}
We implement all models in Stan, which uses the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS; \citealp{hoffman-gelman-nuts}), a self-tuning variant of HMC, well-suited for high-dimensional posteriors. The key improvement is a \emph{non-centered} reparameterization of Model B: \(b_i^{\text{raw}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)\),  \(b_i = \sigma_b \cdot b_i^{\text{raw}}\), decoupling the sampling of \(b_i\) and \(\sigma_b\). Combined with the PC prior on \(\sigma_b\), this yields substantially better mixing for \(\sigma_b\) and \(b_i\). 
For both models, we run 4 chains with 500 warmup and 2,000 sampling iterations, yielding 8,000 posterior samples in CmdStanR \citep{cmdstanr} due to \texttt{rstan}'s extra overhead. 

For the sensitivity sweep, full posterior sampling with HMC for the 7 total \(\gamma\) values would require 11 separate HMC runs, and given the massive scale of the person-period dataset (\(R = 509,944\) rows), this is computationally prohibitive. We use Stan's Automatic Differentiation Variational Inference (ADVI) (full-rank) \citep{cmdstan_guide}, which approximates the posterior with a tractable family and iteratively optimizes the parameters to minimize the KL divergence to the true posterior. While ADVI underestimates heavy-tail uncertainty, it is sufficient for identifying the tipping points where the 95\% credible intervals first cross zero and is orders of magnitude faster than HMC. The HMC results for the standard model (Model~A) at \(\gamma = 0\) serve as the gold standard, ADVI results across the \(\gamma\) grid provide a computationally efficient sensitivity analysis.

\subsubsection{Model B (Frailty).}

Including frailty uniformly amplified  covariate effects, all 17 coefficients moved awau from zero (Table~\ref{tab:beta-comparison}), with the largest increasee for mechanical ventilation (+0.28), sepsis (+0.20), malignancy (+0.19), and emergency admission (+0.17). The correction from the frailty model shows that the standard model conflates unobserved heterogeneity with baseline hazard, biasing fixed effects toward zero.

\section{Derivation of Biased Hazard under Informative Censoring} \label{app:biased-hazard}
At time \(t\) the full population of survivors decomposes into the risk set \(\mathcal{R}_t = \{i: T_i \geq t, C_i \geq t\}\) and the censored set \(\mathcal{C}_t = \{i: T_i \geq t, C_i < t\}\). The  true marginal hazard \(h^\ast_t = \mathbb{E}[h_{i,t} \mid T_i \geq t]\) and observed hazard \(\hat{h}_t = \mathbb{E}[h_{i,t} \mid i \in \mathcal{R}_t]\) are related by:
\begin{align*}
    h^\ast_t &= \Pr(T_i = t \mid T_i \geq t)  
            = \mathbb{E}(h_{i,t} \mid T_i \geq t)\\
    \hat{h}_t &= \Pr(T_i = t \mid T_i \geq t, C_i \geq t)  
            = \mathbb{E}(h_{i,t} \mid T_i \geq t, C_i \geq t) 
            = \mathbb{E}(h_{i,t} \mid i \in \mathcal{R}_t)
\end{align*} 
\noindent
Where \(\lambda_t = \Pr(C_i < T_i \mid T_i \geq t) = |\mathcal{C}_t|/|\mathcal{R}_t|\) as the proportion of survivors (censored at \(t\)) relative to the risk set. Then, \(\hat{h}_t\) can be expressed as a mixture of the hazards in \(\mathcal{R}_t\) and \(\mathcal{C}_t\). Let \(h^c_t = \mathbb{E}(h_{i,t} \mid i \in \mathcal{C}_t)\) be the mean hazard among those censored by \(t\). Then:
\begin{align*}
    h^\ast_t &= (1-\lambda_t) \underbrace{ 
            \mathbb{E}(h_{i,t} \mid i \in \mathcal{R}_t) 
        }_{\hat{h}_t} 
        + \lambda_t \underbrace{ 
            \mathbb{E}(h_{i,t} \mid i \in \mathcal{C}_t)
        }_{h^c_t}
    = (1 - \lambda_t) \hat{h}_t + \lambda_t h^c_t
    \implies   \hat{h}_t &= \frac{h^\ast_t - \lambda_t h^c_t}{1 - \lambda_t}
\end{align*}
Defining \(\Delta_t := h^\ast_t - h^c_t\) and rearranging: \(\hat{h}_t = h^\ast_t + \lambda_t \Delta_t / (1 - \lambda_t)\). The bias is: 
\begin{alignat*}{1}
    \hat{h}_t &= \frac{h^\ast_t - \lambda_t (h^\ast_t - \Delta_t)}{1 - \lambda_t} 
             = \frac{h^\ast_t - \lambda_t h^\ast_t + \lambda_t \Delta_t}{1 - \lambda_t} 
             = h^\ast_t + \frac{\lambda_t \Delta_t}{1 - \lambda_t}\\
\therefore \mathrm{Bias}(\hat{h}_t) &= \hat{h}_t - h^\ast_t = \frac{\lambda_t \Delta_t}{1 - \lambda_t} > 0 \label{eq:bias-formula} 
\end{alignat*}
The bias is positive whenever \(\lambda_t, \Delta_t > 0\), meaning healthier patients censored earlier and compounds the overestimation of the hazard (\(\hat{S}_t = \prod_{s=1}^t (1 - \hat{h}_s)\)). Where as when \(\Delta_t < 0\) the bias is negative, meaning sicker patients censored earlier in the case of hospice discharges. The bias would theoretically underestimate the hazard though this is not the norm in the ICU data (See Figure~\ref{fig:censoring-pattern}).

% censoring rate is higher for healthier/sicker patients; \(\lambda_t \to 1\) means more discharges implies risk set not representative (greater selection bias). For \(\Delta_t\) greater differential among censored vs. risk set patients, greater selection bias, where this compounds in \(\hat{S}_t = \prod_{s=1}^t (1 - \hat{h}_s)\) so underestimation of \(S^\ast_t\) grows with \(t\).

\section{Semiparametric Estimation Equations under Informative Censoring} \label{app:scharfstein1999}

Reiterating a key result from \citet{scharfstein1999} that pertains to this project, censoring induced confounding bias is a failure of the semiparametric estimation equations (score) for \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) when the MAR assumption is violated. When the probability of censoring depends on the unobserved outcome (the failure time \(T_i\)), this constitutes a Missing Not at Random (MNAR) or nonignorable censoring mechanism. In this case, the standard score functions used to estimate \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) are biased proportional to the dependence between \(T_i\) and \(C_i\) given \(\bx_i\).

Let \(\Delta_i = \ind(T_i \le C_i)\) be the observation indicator (0 if censored, 1 if observed). A standard solution under MAR to account for missing data is to use an Inverse Probability Weighting (IPW). If MAR holds, the probability of being observed depends only on \(\bx_i\), denoted \(\pi(\bx_i; \alpha = 0) = \Pr(\Delta_i = 1 \mid \bx_i)\). When MAR is violated, the true probability of being observed depends on both \(T_i\) and \(\bx_i\), denoted \(\pi(T_i, \bx_i; \alpha) = \Pr(\Delta_i = 1 \mid T_i, \bx_i)\). Again, \(\alpha\) is a non-identifiable selection parameter that indexes the severity of the MAR violation.

In standard parametric estimation, the expectation of the score at the true parameter value \(\boldsymbol{\beta}_0\) always equals zero. Under informative censoring, however, this is no longer the case. To find the bias, we take the expectation under the MNAR distribution:

\begin{align*}
    \mathrm{Bias}(\alpha)
    &= \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{ \Delta_i }{\pi(\bx_i; 0)} S_\text{full}(\boldsymbol{\beta}_0) \right] 
    = \mathbb{E} \left[ \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{ \Delta_i  }{\pi(\bx_i; 0)} S_\text{full} (\boldsymbol{\beta}_0) \bigg| T_i, \bx_i \right] \right]
    = \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{\pi(T_i, \bx_i; \alpha)}{\pi(\bx_i; 0)} S_\text{full}(\boldsymbol{\beta}_0) \right]
\end{align*}
where \(S_\text{full}(\boldsymbol{\beta})\) is the full-data score function (i.e., the score function we would have if we observed \(T_i\) for all patients). Following \citet{scharfstein1999}, if we specify the conditional hazard of censoring as \(\lambda_C(t \mid \bx_i, T_i) = \lambda_0(t \mid \bx_i)\exp(\alpha T_i)\), where \(\lambda_0\) is the baseline hazard of censoring, and \(\exp(\alpha T_i)\) is the ``tilt'' caused by unobserved dependence between \(T_i\) and \(C_i\). The survival function of censoring at \(T_i\): \(\pi(T_i, \bx_i; \alpha) = \exp\{ - \int_0^{T_i} \lambda_C(u \mid \bx_i, T_i) du \}  = \exp\{ - \int_0^{T_i} \lambda_0(u \mid \bx_i) \exp(\alpha T_i) du \} = \exp\{ - \exp(\alpha T_i) \Lambda_0(T_i \mid \bx_i) \}\), where \(\Lambda_0\) is the cumulative baseline hazard of censoring. While under MAR, \(\pi(\bx_i; 0) = \exp\{ - \Lambda_0(T_i \mid \bx_i) \}\). Thus, the ratio of the true to assumed selection probabilities is:
\begin{align*}
    \frac{\pi(T_i, \bx_i; \alpha)}{\pi(\bx_i; 0)} 
    &= \frac{\exp\{ - \exp(\alpha T_i) \Lambda_0(T_i \mid \bx_i) \}}{\exp\{ - \Lambda_0(T_i \mid \bx_i) \}} \\
    &\approx \frac{\exp\{ - (1 + \alpha T_i) \Lambda_0(T_i \mid \bx_i) \}}{\exp\{ - \Lambda_0(T_i \mid \bx_i) \}}\\
     &= \exp\{ - \alpha T_i \Lambda_0(T_i \mid \bx_i) \}
\end{align*}
where \(e^{\alpha T_i} \approx 1 + \alpha T_i\) in the tilt term on the numerator. Applying a first-order Taylor expansion for small \(\alpha\) yields:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\pi(T_i, \bx_i; \alpha)}{\pi(\bx_i; 0)} \approx 1 - \alpha T_i \Lambda_0(T_i \mid \bx_i)
\end{equation}
Substituting this back into the expectation yields the explicit bias approximation:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Bias}(\alpha) \approx -\alpha \, \mathbb{E} \Big[ \Lambda_0(T_i \mid \bx_i) \, T_i \, S_\text{full}(\boldsymbol{\beta}) \Big]
\end{equation}

This result explicitly demonstrates the main theoretical consequence of informative censoring: the bias in estimating \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) is strictly driven by \(\alpha\) (the severity of the MAR violation/selection) and the covariance (cumulative hazard) between the failure time \(T_i\) and the censoring \(\Lambda_0(T_i \mid \bx_i)\). Because \(\alpha\) cannot be identified from the observed data alone, standard estimation fails, motivating the need for alternative approaches such as sensitivity analyses or frailty models (as discussed in Section \ref{sc:frailty}) to absorb this unobserved dependence.

\section{Simulation Study} \label{app:simulation}
We validate the methodology with synthetic ICU data ($N \in \{300, 500\}$, $K_{\max} = 90$, $\Delta \in \{1, 3, 7, 14\}$) where informative censoring is known: $\text{logit}(h^c_{i,t}) = -2.5 - 0.3\log(t) - 0.8 b_i$, so healthier patients (lower $b_i$) have higher discharge probability ($\sigma_{b,\text{true}} = 0.6$). Key findings: (i) Model~A reliably converges with zero divergences and ESS $> 5{,}000$ across all configurations. (ii) Model~B's $\sigma_b$ is weakly identified: ESS $\approx 80$--150, $\hat{R} \approx 1.03$--1.08, and 95\% CrI spanning [0.016, 1.660] with truth 0.600. LOO-CV (not implemented in main analysis due to computational constraints) never favors Model~B. (iii) The sensitivity analysis converges cleanly across $\gamma \in [-2, 2]$ and correctly distinguishes robust from fragile covariate effects. (iv) Model~A calibration shows systematic over-prediction consistent with \eqref{eq:bias-approx}.
